What is Partner 360?

Partner 360 is a single screen that gives a complete, real-time view of any distributor in your network. Pick a state, pick a partner — and you see everything: revenue health, churn risk, how much money you're leaving on the table, their credit exposure, and a ready-to-use sales script tailored to their data.

Every number is computed live by the ML engine from the production database. Nothing is manually maintained or hardcoded. The data flow looks like this:

PostgreSQL Database ↓ BaseLoaderMixin → Revenue, recency, growth, volatility features ↓ ChurnCreditMixin → Churn probability + Credit risk score ClusteringMixin → Partner segments (which "type" of buyer are they?) AssociationsMixin → Product affinities (what to cross-sell?) LifecycleMixin → Is this product growing or dying? ↓ RecommendationMixin → Ranked actions + Confidence scores ↓ Partner 360 UI → Sales rep sees everything in one screen

01

Health Score

base_loader_mixin.py → _add_health_scores()

A single number between 0 and 1 summarising how well a partner is doing right now. Drives the traffic-light segmentation shown at the top of every partner card.

Segmentation Thresholds

SegmentHealth ScoreRevenue DropWhat it means
Champion≥ 0.80< 10%Growing, healthy, no intervention needed
Healthy≥ 0.60< thresholdStable, monitor quarterly
At Risk≥ 0.40AnyDeclining — needs attention within 2 weeks
Critical< 0.40AnyImmediate action or revenue is already zero

The Formula

Health Score = 0.35 × revenue_strength ← How much they spend + 0.30 × growth_trend ← Are they growing or shrinking? + 0.20 × recency_activity ← How recently did they order? + 0.15 × stability ← Is their revenue consistent? ── Each component normalised 0→1 before combining ── revenue_strength = normalise( log(1 + recent_90d_revenue) ) growth_trend = normalise( growth_rate_90d clipped to [-100%, +150%] ) recency_activity = 1 − normalise( log(1 + recency_days) ) stability = 1 − normalise( log(1 + revenue_volatility) )
🔍
Why log-transform recency? The old version used raw recency_days ÷ 365. That meant a partner absent for 10 days looked almost as bad as one absent for 40 days. In reality, a sales rep cares much more about 200 days vs 50 days than 10 days vs 40 days.

Log compression fixes this:
10d → 0.19  |  100d → 0.39  |  300d → 0.52
A 300-day absence now scores twice as urgent as 100 days — which is what the business actually needs.

State-Level Degrowth Threshold

degrowth_threshold = 70th percentile of revenue_drop_pct within the same state (clamped between 10% and 40%)

A 30% drop in Maharashtra is different from a 30% drop in a smaller state where seasonal swings are bigger. Computing the threshold per state from actual data means partners aren't penalised for normal regional fluctuations.

✅ Current ApproachWeighted formula with log-transformed recency. Gives real differentiation across all partners. Explainable to sales reps.
Alternative: RFM ScoreClassic retail approach. Less reliable in B2B where a distributor might order monthly in large batches — low frequency doesn't mean they're at risk.
02

Churn Probability

churn_credit_stub_mixin.py → _score_churn_rule_based()

The probability (0–1) that a partner will stop ordering in the next 90 days. Drives the "Revenue at Risk" metric and the retention recommendation priority.

Churn Risk Bands

BandThresholdRecommended Action
High> 70%Call within 24 hours. Retention intervention.
Medium45–70%Schedule visit this week. Probe for blockers.
Low< 45%Regular cycle. Monitor.

The Scoring Formula

Churn Score = w₁ × rev_drop ← Revenue dropped vs last 90 days + w₂ × log_recency ← Days since last order (log-scaled) + w₃ × CoV ← Revenue unpredictability + w₄ × growth_risk ← Negative growth trend + w₅ × txn_drop ← Transaction count falling ── Individual feature transforms ── rev_drop = revenue_drop_pct ÷ 100 (clipped 0–1) log_recency = log(1 + recency_days) ÷ log(1 + 730) (clipped 0–1) CoV = revenue_volatility ÷ recent_90d_revenue (normalised 0–1) growth_risk = (1 − growth_rate_90d) ÷ 2 (clipped 0–1) txn_drop = (prev_txns − recent_txns) ÷ prev_txns (clipped 0–1) ── Special rule: zero recent revenue → force churn = 0.85 ──

How Weights Are Calibrated (Not Hardcoded)

The weights w₁–w₅ are computed fresh every session using point-biserial correlation — a standard statistical technique for measuring how a continuous variable relates to a binary outcome.

Step 1: Generate churn labels from 12–15 months of history Active in month M + zero revenue in month M+3 → churn_label = 1 Active and still ordering → churn_label = 0 Step 2: Compute correlation between each feature and label corr(feature, churn_label) using Pearson (= point-biserial for binary y) Step 3: Normalise to weights that sum to 1.0 w_feature = |corr(feature, label)| ÷ Σ|corr(all features, label)| Fallback if <20 samples or <5 churn events: {rev_drop: 0.30, recency: 0.25, growth: 0.20, volatility: 0.15, txn_drop: 0.10}

Revenue at Risk Formula

Revenue at Risk (90d) = churn_probability × recent_90d_revenue Revenue at Risk (monthly) = churn_probability × recent_90d_revenue ÷ 3 Example: 60% churn risk, Rs 5L revenue → Rs 3L at risk
⚖️
Why not XGBoost? (We built it but don't use it) We trained XGBoost on historical data, but when scoring active partners it predicted ~1% churn for almost everyone. The problem: it was trained on "zero revenue = churned", and all active partners have some revenue. Gradient boosted trees learn the label exactly — they don't naturally generalise to "declining but not yet zero." The rule-based scoring with calibrated correlation weights gives far more useful differentiation.
03

30-Day Revenue Forecast

churn_credit_stub_mixin.py → _build_partner_forecast()
Forecast_30d = (recent_90d_revenue ÷ 3) × (1 + growth_rate_90d ÷ 3) Confidence = 0.75 if partner has ≥ 6 months history = 0.45 if newer partner
📊
Why not Prophet or ARIMA? Monthly B2B order data is lumpy — a distributor may place one large order in January and nothing until March. ARIMA would try to fit a pattern that doesn't exist. Prophet handles seasonality well but needs 2+ years of clean, dense data. Until we have proper model validation pipelines, linear projection is honest and explainable. The database query for full time-series history already exists — upgrading to Prophet is an execution priority, not a technical blocker.

Partner Clustering (Segmentation)

clustering_mixin.py

Groups all partners into business segments based on purchasing behaviour. This is what makes peer comparison possible — you can only benchmark a partner against similar partners if you first know which group they belong to.

Pipeline: PCA → UMAP → HDBSCAN + GMM

StepAlgorithmWhat it doesKey parameter
1Feature groupingGroups columns by category (spend, RFM, etc.) with weightsGroup weight = 1.0 (spend), 0.8 (RFM)
2PCACompresses correlated features within each groupn_components = min(features, n÷6, 8)
3UMAPNon-linear dimensionality reduction to find curved cluster shapesn_neighbors, min_dist (adaptive)
4HDBSCAN + GMMTwo algorithms compete — best Silhouette Score winsAutomatic cluster count
5VIP classificationTop 80th percentile revenue + revenue share checkVIP contribution: 20–45% of total

Adaptive UMAP Parameters

n_neighbors = max(5, min(30, √n_partners)) → √n gives statistically sound local-global balance → For 100 partners: n_neighbors = 10 → For 400 partners: n_neighbors = 20 min_dist: < 50 partners → 0.10 (spread out — small datasets compress too much) 50–200 partners → 0.05 (balanced — typical B2B size) 200+ partners → 0.01 (tighter — large data has denser structure)

The old version hardcoded n_neighbors=15, min_dist=0.10, which worked at 100 partners but produced overlapping clusters at 300+. The square-root formula is the standard recommendation from the UMAP paper authors.

VIP + High spend + Concentrated in one categoryCategory Champions
VIP + Medium spend + DiversifiedEmerging Power Buyers

Peer Gap Analysis — Cross-Sell Identification

Compares this partner's spend in each product category against the average of their cluster peers. A gap = a cross-sell opportunity with a specific Rs value attached to it.

Gap % = Others_Do_Pct − You_Do_Pct (floored at 0) Potential (monthly) = Gap % × Peer_Avg_Monthly_Spend_In_Category Potential (yearly) = Potential Monthly × 12

Priority Tiers

PriorityMonthly GapDisplay
🔥 High> ₹50,000/monthShown upfront — immediate pitch opportunity
⚡ Medium₹10,000 – ₹50,000Shown — worth a structured conversation
Low< ₹10,000Collapsed — mention in passing only
💡
Why compare only within the cluster?A small regional distributor should never be benchmarked against a national key account. Clustering ensures the comparison is always like-for-like. If cluster peers average ₹50K/month on smartphones and this partner spends ₹10K, the ₹40K gap is realistic because the peers are genuinely similar in size and profile.

Product Affinity — FP-Growth Association Rules

associations_mixin.py

Finds products that are frequently bought together. If 70% of partners who buy Product A also buy Product B that same month, we should pitch B to any partner who buys A but not B.

Key Metrics

MetricFormulaOur ThresholdWhat it means
Support% of baskets containing both A and BAdaptive (see below)How common is this pairing?
ConfidenceP(B | A) — given A, how often is B also bought?≥ 15%How reliable is this recommendation?
LiftP(A∩B) ÷ (P(A) × P(B))≥ 1.1Is this better than random? (>1 = yes)

Adaptive Min-Support (Key Fix)

The original code used a static min_support = 2%. In sparse B2B data (200 partners, 50 products, each buying 3–5 products/month), this eliminated many real patterns. The new formula:

sparsity = actual_pair_co-occurrences ÷ max_possible_pair_co-occurrences if sparsity < 0.10: min_support = max(0.005, 2 ÷ n_baskets) ← min 2 real occurrences if sparsity 0.10–0.30: min_support = 0.01 if sparsity > 0.30: min_support = 0.02 ← standard default

The floor of 2/n_baskets ensures we never report a "rule" based on a single coincidence — a minimum of 2 actual co-occurrences is always required.

Credit Risk Scoring

churn_credit_stub_mixin.py → _load_credit_risk_features()

Three-Tier Logic (Best Available Data Wins)

TierSourceWhen UsedAccuracy
Tier 1view_partner_credit_risk_score (AR view)When view has real overdue data (max score > 0.05)⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Ground truth
Tier 2due_payment table — payment age signalsWhen AR view returns all zeros⭐⭐⭐⭐ Leading indicator
Tier 3Revenue proxy onlyWhen due_payment is also unavailable⭐⭐ Lagging indicator

Tier 2 Formula — Enhanced Payment Proxy

Credit Risk = 0.35 × payment_delay_ratio ← Leading: are they paying late? + 0.25 × overdue_outstanding_norm ← Confirmed: unpaid amount / revenue + 0.20 × concentration_risk ← Fragility: single-category dependency + 0.20 × rev_drop_pct ← Lagging: revenue already falling payment_delay_ratio = (avg_payment_days − assigned_credit_days) ÷ assigned_credit_days overdue_outstanding_norm = unpaid_amount ÷ recent_90d_revenue concentration_risk = 1 ÷ category_count (1 category = 1.0 risk)
⏰
Why payment_delay_ratio gets 35% weight — the leading vs lagging distinctionRevenue drop is a lagging signal: by the time revenue drops, the credit event has often already happened or is imminent. Payment age fires early — a partner consistently paying 15 days past their 30-day credit window is already showing financial stress. We want to catch it before it becomes a bad debt, not after.

Credit Adjusted Risk Value

Credit Adjusted Risk Value = credit_risk_score × recent_90d_revenue Example: 80% risk score, Rs 5L revenue → Rs 4L credit adjusted risk This is the money-weighted number — far more useful for collections prioritisation.

Product Lifecycle Staging

product_lifecycle_mixin.py

Classifies every product into a lifecycle stage. Sales reps should pitch Growing products aggressively, defend Mature ones, and start phasing out End-of-Life items.

Velocity Score Formula

Velocity Score = 0.35 × (slope_per_month_pct ÷ 10) ← Linear trend slope + 0.30 × (growth_3m_pct ÷ 50) ← Recent 3-month growth + 0.20 × (buyer_trend ÷ 2) ← Number of buyers trending + 0.15 × (txn_trend ÷ 5) ← Transaction count trending slope_per_month comes from np.polyfit() — best-fit line through monthly revenue history

Data-Driven Stage Boundaries

OLD (fixed thresholds — broken when all products are declining): velocity ≥ 0.3 → Growing ← What if no product reaches 0.3? NEW (percentile-based — adapts to this dataset's actual distribution): v_p80 = 80th percentile of velocity scores → Growing floor v_p50 = median → Mature floor v_p25 = 25th percentile → Declining start v_p10 = 10th percentile → End-of-Life ceiling

Slope + Acceleration Signals

acceleration = growth_3m_pct − growth_rate_90d × 100 Positive = product is speeding up (3m better than 6m) → momentum Negative = product is decelerating (3m worse than 6m) → plateauing
StageClassification Conditions
Growingv ≥ v_p80 AND growth ≥ g_p70 AND acceleration ≥ 0
Maturev ≥ v_p50 AND growth ≥ g_p30 AND peak distance < 20%
Plateauingv ≥ v_p25 AND peak distance < 30% AND acceleration < 0
Decliningv ≥ v_p10 OR growth < g_p30
End-of-Lifev < v_p10 AND recency > 180 days
