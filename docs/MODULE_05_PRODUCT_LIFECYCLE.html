Not all products are equal — and not all declines are the same. A product might be declining because a newer model cannibalized it, or because demand is genuinely dying, or simply because it hasn't been marketed. This module tells you which products are growing, which are dying, and how fast — so procurement, marketing, and sales decisions are based on data instead of gut feel. The Five Lifecycle Stages

Every product in the catalogue is classified into one of five stages. This is not a manual categorisation — it's computed automatically every time the engine runs based on revenue trajectory, growth momentum, and distance from the product's peak revenue.

Velocity Score — The Master Signal

product_lifecycle_mixin.py → _compute_growth_velocity()

The velocity score is a single number that summarises a product's current momentum. It combines four independent signals, each capturing a different aspect of how a product is performing. The range is −1 (maximum decline) to +1 (maximum growth).

velocity_score = 0.35 × clip(slope_pct / 10, −1, 1) ← Revenue trend slope + 0.30 × clip(growth_3m / 50, −1, 1) ← 3-month growth rate + 0.20 × clip(buyer_trend / 2, −1, 1) ← Buyer count trend + 0.15 × clip(txn_trend / 5, −1, 1) ← Transaction intensity trend Each component is clipped to [−1, +1] before weighting. Prevents any single explosive signal from dominating the composite score.

Why include buyer trend and transaction trend, not just revenue? Revenue can be misleading. A product might show rising revenue simply because prices went up — but if the number of unique buyers is declining, the product is actually weakening. Including buyer trend (are more or fewer companies ordering this?) and transaction intensity (how often per period?) gives a more complete picture of true demand health.
03

Linear Regression Slope — Revenue Trend Direction

product_lifecycle_mixin.py → np.polyfit(month_idx, revenue, deg=1)

The slope is computed using ordinary least squares linear regression on the monthly revenue series. It tells us: on average, how much does revenue change each month?

For each product with at least 3 months of data: sub["month_idx"] = [0, 1, 2, ..., N-1] ← numerical month index coeffs = np.polyfit(month_idx, monthly_revenue, deg=1) slope = coeffs[0] ← Rs per month change in revenue slope_pct = (slope ÷ avg_monthly_revenue) × 100 normalised slope as % of average revenue per month Example: slope = +₹25,000/month, avg_monthly = ₹1,00,000 → slope_pct = +25% slope = −₹10,000/month, avg_monthly = ₹50,000 → slope_pct = −20% Peak distance: peak_distance_pct = (peak_revenue - current_revenue) ÷ peak_revenue × 100 0% = currently at peak | 80% = 80% below its historical high Used in End-of-Life classification to detect products far from their peak

3M Growth Rate

recent_3 = sum of last 3 months revenue prior_3 = sum of months 4–6 before the last (or earliest available) growth_3m_pct = (recent_3 - prior_3) ÷ prior_3 × 100 This is a period-over-period (POP) rate, not annualised. Positive = recent 3M is better than the prior 3M period. Negative = recent 3M is worse → early warning flag.
04

Revenue Volatility — Coefficient of Variation

A product might have a positive slope but still be worrying if its revenue swings wildly from month to month. The coefficient of variation (CV) captures this unpredictability.

CV = std_deviation(monthly_revenue) ÷ mean(monthly_revenue) Interpretation: CV = 0.10 → revenue varies by ±10% of mean — very stable CV = 0.50 → revenue varies by ±50% of mean — high volatility CV > 0.80 → revenue is erratic — may indicate single-buyer dependency Used in stage classification (Plateauing: slope ≈ 0 AND high volatility) Also shown in UI as "Volatility (CV)" column for management awareness
💡
High volatility + Mature stage = concentration risk If a product is classified as Mature (stable revenue, positive velocity) but has a CV > 0.6, it probably means one or two large partners are keeping it alive. The moment they reduce purchases, the product will drop to Declining immediately. This is a procurement risk that the raw stage label alone doesn't show — the CV column exposes it.
05

Data-Driven Percentile Thresholds — Why Not Fixed Numbers?

product_lifecycle_mixin.py → lines 223–285

The original version used hardcoded thresholds: velocity >= 0.3 = Growing, velocity < -0.4 = End-of-Life. This created a serious problem: if one year all products are declining, the fixed threshold would push every product into the same "End-of-Life" bucket. Conversely, in a growth year everything looks "Growing."

The current system computes the thresholds from the actual distribution of velocity scores in this specific dataset, on this specific run.

Compute percentile boundaries from this run's velocity scores: v_p80 = 80th percentile of velocity_score → Growing threshold v_p50 = 50th percentile (median) → Mature threshold v_p25 = 25th percentile → Plateauing threshold v_p10 = 10th percentile → EOL threshold g_p70 = 70th percentile of growth_3m_pct → "top 30% growth" line g_p30 = 30th percentile of growth_3m_pct → "weak growth" line Safety clamps (prevents degenerate boundaries): v_p80 = max(v_p80, 0.05) ← Growing must have at least positive velocity v_p10 = min(v_p10, -0.05) ← EOL must genuinely be negative momentum Classification logic (first matching rule wins): End-of-Life : v < v_p10 AND recency_days > 180 Growing : v ≥ v_p80 AND g ≥ g_p70 AND acceleration ≥ 0 Mature : v ≥ v_p50 AND g ≥ g_p30 AND peak_dist < 20% Plateauing : v ≥ v_p25 AND peak_dist < 30% AND acceleration < 0 Declining : all remaining cases with negative momentum End-of-Life : fallback for anything left

Acceleration — The New Signal

As part of the recent enhancement, the classifier now uses an acceleration signal — the difference between short-term growth (3M) and medium-term growth (90d). This is the "derivative of growth" — not just whether growth is positive, but whether growth is getting faster or slower.

acceleration = growth_3m_pct - growth_rate_90d_as_pct acceleration > 0 → momentum is increasing (accelerating) Used to confirm Growing stage: must be accelerating, not just positive acceleration < 0 → momentum is slowing (decelerating) Even if velocity is above median, deceleration signals Plateauing Origin: borrowed from physics — a car moving fast but braking hard is about to be slower than a car moving slowly but accelerating.

Cannibalization Detection

product_lifecycle_mixin.py → _detect_cannibalization()

Cannibalization is when one product's growth comes at the expense of a related product — typically because customers are substituting the newer/cheaper/better product for the old one. Left undetected, it causes a common mistake: companies over-invest in a declining product thinking "the market shifted" when actually their own catalogue is eating it.

Algorithm: 1. Get the set of Growing products (stage = "Growing") 2. Get the set of Declining/EOL products 3. For each association rule in MBA results: prod_a → prod_b OR prod_b → prod_a 4. Flag a pair if: - One product is Growing AND the other is Declining/EOL - Association confidence ≥ 0.15 (products are genuinely linked) - Lift ≥ 1.0 (association is above random) 5. Compute: cannibalization_score = velocity(cannibal) - velocity(victim) Higher score = stronger substitution evidence estimated_revenue_shift = |slope_per_month(victim)| × 3 Estimated revenue shifted from victim to cannibal in last 3 months
⚠️
Important caveat: correlation, not causation Cannibalization detection uses two signals: (1) that the products were frequently bought together and (2) that one is growing while the other declines. This is a strong correlation signal — but not proof of causation. Two products bought together might decline together due to an external market factor, not because one is replacing the other. The table should always be reviewed by a product manager who understands the catalogue context before drawing procurement conclusions.

End-of-Life Risk Score

product_lifecycle_mixin.py → _predict_end_of_life()

For products in Declining, Plateauing, or End-of-Life stages, the engine computes an EOL risk score from 0 to 1 and estimates how many months remain before revenue reaches zero based on the current trajectory.

Four components, each scored 0.0 → 1.0: Component 1 — Revenue decline speed (weight: 30%) IF slope < 0: score = min(|slope| / current_revenue × 10, 1.0) IF slope ≥ 0: score = 0 (not declining, no speed risk) Component 2 — Distance from peak (weight: 25%) score = min(peak_distance_pct / 100, 1.0) 80% below peak → score = 0.80 Component 3 — Buyer attrition (weight: 25%) IF buyer_trend < 0: score = min(|buyer_trend| / 3, 1.0) IF buyer_trend ≥ 0: score = 0 (still gaining buyers) Component 4 — Stock age risk (weight: 20%) score = min(max_age_days / 180, 1.0) Stock older than 180 days → maximum aging risk eol_risk_score = 0.30×C1 + 0.25×C2 + 0.25×C3 + 0.20×C4 Estimated months to zero revenue (linear extrapolation): months_to_zero = current_revenue ÷ |slope_per_month| (only if slope < 0) capped at 36 months maximum

EOL Urgency Classification & Clearance Action Generator

The risk score is translated into an urgency level, which then drives a specific suggested action and enables the clearance deal generator.

🔴 Critical
Risk ≥ 0.70 OR months_to_zero ≤ 3
Liquidate immediately. Stop all procurement. Run immediate clearance sale.
🟠 High
Risk ≥ 0.45 OR months_to_zero ≤ 6
Reduce reorder quantity. Bundle with fast-movers. Discount 15–25%.
🟡 Medium
Risk ≥ 0.25 OR months_to_zero ≤ 12
Monitor closely. Create promotional bundles. Limit new purchases.
🟢 Low
Risk < 0.25 AND months_to_zero > 12
Continue monitoring. Review in 3 months.

Prophet Forecasting — Time-Series with Seasonality

product_lifecycle_mixin.py → _prophet_forecast_product()

The Product Lifecycle module is the only place in the system that uses a proper time-series forecasting model. Prophet (by Meta/Facebook) was built specifically for business time-series with seasonal patterns — monthly, quarterly, and yearly cycles — which is exactly the shape of product sales data in a B2B distribution context.

✅ Prophet (Primary Method)
  • Captures yearly seasonality (festival season spikes)
  • Handles trend changepoints (when a product "turned the corner")
  • Provides uncertainty intervals (yhat_lower / yhat_upper at 80% CI)
  • Automatically decomposes trend vs seasonality
  • Used when: ≥ 6 months of data available
→ Linear Extrapolation (Fallback)
  • Simple np.polyfit line projected forward
  • No seasonality or uncertainty bounds (uses ±15% band)
  • Fast and dependency-free
  • Used when: < 6 months of data OR prophet not installed
  • Suitable for early-stage products with short history

Prophet Configuration

Prophet model parameters (justified choices): m = Prophet( yearly_seasonality = True, ← Captures Diwali / Q4 demand spikes weekly_seasonality = False, ← Monthly data has no weekly signal daily_seasonality = False, ← Monthly aggregation, not daily seasonality_mode = "multiplicative", ← Seasonal effect scales with level changepoint_prior_scale = 0.1, ← Moderate flexibility (0.05=rigid, 0.5=overfit) interval_width = 0.80, ← 80% confidence interval (not 95%) ) Why multiplicative seasonality? Additive: seasonal effect is fixed ±₹N regardless of revenue level Multiplicative: seasonal effect is proportional (±X% of current level) B2B products typically show % swings, not fixed ₹ swings → multiplicative fits better Why 80% CI instead of 95%? 95% intervals are very wide for short time-series, making the chart visually uninformative. 80% CI is tighter and more actionable for planning. Output: forecast rows for periods=6 months ahead Minimum data requirement: 6 months (< 6 → linear fallback)
