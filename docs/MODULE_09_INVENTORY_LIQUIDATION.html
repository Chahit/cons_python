Unsold inventory is silent money. Every unit sitting in a warehouse is working capital that can't be deployed elsewhere — and in a distribution business handling lubricants, paints, and construction chemicals, stock doesn't stay fresh forever. This module does two things that most inventory systems don't: it tells you not just what is dead stock, but who should buy it — using past purchase history and ML-derived cluster lookalike audiences to build a ready-to-use outreach list that your sales team can download and call from the same dashboard.

Data Sources — Two Stock Views

The Inventory module draws from two pre-computed database views. Neither is computed at runtime — both are materialised views in the database that summarise stock position and buyer history. This makes the tab fast even for large product catalogues.

🗄️
Why use pre-computed views rather than querying transactions directly? Computing stock age from raw goods-receipt and sales transaction tables on every page load would require a complex multi-table join across potentially millions of rows. By using a materialised view, the heavy SQL aggregation runs once (typically nightly or on a schedule) and results are served instantly. The tradeoff is that stock position could be up to 24 hours old — acceptable for a weekly clearance planning workflow, but would need real-time joins for live warehouse systems.
02

Age-Bucket Classification

inventory.py → Portfolio Ageing Distribution

Every unit of stock is classified into one of four age buckets based on how long it has been in the warehouse. The buckets prioritise urgency — old stock in the 90+ days band is the most urgent liquidation target.

0–30 Days
Fresh stock. Normal replenishment range. No action needed.
31–60 Days
Watch list. Start monitoring sell-through velocity – if slow, prepare bundling.
61–90 Days
Action needed. Initiate targeted outreach to past buyers. Apply promotional pricing.
90+ Days
Dead stock. Liquidate immediately. Flash sale, clearance bundle, or write-off planning.

Two Bucketing Methods — With Automatic Fallback

Method 1 (preferred): Pre-bucketed columns from the DB view IF age_0_30, age_31_60, age_61_90, age_90_plus exist in df_stock_stats: → Use them directly: sum each column across all SKUs to get bucket totals → Most accurate: DB view computes per-batch age at source Method 2 (fallback): Derive buckets from max_age_days if pre-bucketed cols missing IF only max_age_days is available: → Use max_age_days as a proxy for the oldest open batch for that SKU → Classify each SKU into a bucket based on its maximum warehouse age → Count units (total_stock_qty) per bucket Fallback logic: age_0_30 = sum(qty) WHERE max_age_days <= 30 age_31_60 = sum(qty) WHERE max_age_days > 30 AND <= 60 age_61_90 = sum(qty) WHERE max_age_days > 60 AND <= 90 age_90plus = sum(qty) WHERE max_age_days > 90 Note: if NO qty column exists, fallback counts SKUs instead of units. (Total SKUs in each bucket rather than total warehouse units.)

State-Wise Dead Stock Heatmap

inventory.py → lines 82–104

When state and category data is available in the dead stock view, the module renders a stacked bar chart showing which states have the highest quantity of at-risk inventory and which product categories are driving it. This is a geographic demand signal — if a state has more dead stock than others, it might simply be that the assigned rep isn't visiting those partners frequently enough, or demand in that region for that category has dropped.

Group logic: state_agg = df_dead.groupby([state_col, category_col])[qty_col].sum() Column detection (fault-tolerant — tries alternate column names): state_col = first found from: ["state", "partner_state", "buyer_state"] cat_col = first found from: ["category", "product_category", "dead_stock_item"] qty_col = first found from: ["buyer_past_purchase_qty", "total_qty", "qty"] Stacked bar chart: X = State, Y = Total Qty at Risk, Color = Category Allows management to see: "Maharashtra has 3,400 units of dead Epoxy Primer" vs. "Gujarat has 800 units of dead Synthetic Enamel" This shapes whether the response is category-wide or territory-specific
04

Stock Exposure Score (0–100)

monitoring_mixin.py → get_stock_details()

For each product selected in the drilldown, a composite exposure score is computed to quantify the financial urgency of clearing that specific item. The score combines four independent signals, each capturing a different dimension of inventory risk.

Four weighted components: age_component = min(max_age_days / 180, 1.0) × 55 Primary signal. Stock at 180 days = maximum age risk contribution (55 pts). 180 days chosen as the "full risk" threshold — beyond this, the stock is typically unrecoverable through normal sales channels in a B2B context. percentile_component = min(age_percentile, 100) / 100 × 20 Relative position of this SKU's age vs all other SKUs in the portfolio. A product at the 90th percentile of age = 18 out of 20 pts. Catches products that are old even for this business's norms. qty_component = min(total_stock_qty / 100, 1.0) × 15 Volume of unsold units. 100+ units = maximum volume risk (15 pts). A product with 500 old units is a bigger problem than one with 5 old units. recency_component = min(demand_recency_days / 180, 1.0) × 10 Days since the last known purchase of this product by any buyer. Only used when demand_recency_days is available from df_dead_stock. 180+ days since last buyer = maximum demand dryness signal (10 pts). stock_exposure_score = age_component + percentile_component + qty_component + recency_component Maximum possible: 55 + 20 + 15 + 10 = 100

💡
Why does stock age carry 55% of the weight? Old inventory is the root problem in distribution — the longer stock sits, the more warehouse space it occupies, the more interest cost it incurs on working capital, and the more it depreciates (especially for seasonal or technical products). A product with 500 units sitting for 10 days is less urgent than one with 20 units sitting for 200 days. The 55% weight on age ensures the score reflects this financial reality rather than treating all large-quantity stock as equally urgent.

How age_percentile is computed

age_series = df_stock_stats["max_age_days"] (cleaned, no nulls) age_percentile = fraction of all SKUs with max_age_days <= this_product's_age × 100 Example: Product X has max_age_days = 120 60% of all SKUs have max_age_days <= 120 → age_percentile = 60.0 → percentile_component = (60/100) × 20 = 12 pts Fallback: if age_series is empty or null, age_percentile = 50.0 (median assumed)

Past-Buyer Lead Generation

inventory.py → leads = df_dead[df_dead["dead_stock_item"] == selected_item]

When a dead stock item is selected, the system pulls all rows from df_dead_stock where dead_stock_item matches. Each row represents a partner who has purchased that product at least once in the past — making them the highest-probability buyer for a clearance offer. The leads are sorted by buyer_past_purchase_qty descending — partners who bought the most of this product historically appear first.

06

Lookalike Audience Discovery — Cluster-Powered

inventory.py → lines 174–191

This is the feature that makes the Inventory module unique. After identifying past buyers, the system identifies which cluster(s) those buyers belong to — then surfaces other partners in the same cluster who have not yet bought this product. These are lookalike audiences: partners who behave like your past buyers, making them likely candidates for the same product.

Past Buyer
Proven Demand — High Priority
Partners who specifically purchased this SKU before. Their historical purchase quantity is shown. These are your warmest leads — they know the product, they've bought it, and the conversation is "we have stock at a discount, are you interested?" Not an education sell.
Lookalike
Cluster-Derived — New Opportunity
Partners in the same cluster as past buyers who haven't bought this product. They share similar revenue profile, product category mix, and buying behaviour with the actual buyers. Sorted by recent_90_revenue (most active partners first) and capped at 10 per cluster.
Lookalike discovery algorithm (step-by-step): 1. Extract cluster labels of all past buyers for this SKU: buyer_clusters = leads["cluster_label"].dropna().unique() 2. Filter the full partner features matrix: lookalikes = df_partner_features WHERE: cluster_label IN buyer_clusters ← same cluster as a real buyer AND company_name NOT IN past_buyers ← hasn't already bought this SKU 3. Sort by recent_90_revenue descending — most active partners first 4. Take top 10 lookalikes (prevents overwhelming the leads table) 5. Append to leads table with: potential_buyer = company_name mobile_no = "Lookalike" (not in dead_stock view, shown as a label) buyer_past_purchase_qty = 0 (no prior purchase of this SKU) last_purchase_date = "Never" Audience Type = "Lookalike (Cluster: VIP Stable)" etc. Combined table: past buyers sorted first (high qty DESC), then lookalikes appended Sales team strategy: call past buyers first, then lookalikes as expansion targets
ℹ️
Why use cluster membership as the lookalike signal instead of product category overlap? Category overlap tells you "this partner buys paints, therefore pitch Epoxy Primer" — which is too broad. Cluster membership tells you "this partner behaves like the three specific partners who actually bought this exact dead stock item." The clustering algorithm has already found multi-dimensional similarity covering revenue level, buying frequency, category mix, and recency. It's a richer, more specific signal than a single category match.
07

Capital Locked Calculation

inventory.py → lines 124–140

One of the four KPI cards on the product detail view shows the total capital currently locked in the unsold stock of the selected item. This turns a warehouse unit count into a rupee figure that finance and management can act on.

capital_locked = total_stock_qty × cost_price Cost price resolution order (with fallback chain): 1. stock_details.get("cost_price") ← from df_stock_stats view (preferred) 2. df_master_products lookup by product_name ← master product catalogue - Tries column names: ["cost_price", "unit_cost", "purchase_price"] 3. Fallback: cost_price = Rs 1,000 (estimate) cost_label = " (est.)" ← shown in UI to signal it's approximate The fallback prevents a division error or missing value from blocking the UI. The "(est.)" label ensures transparency — management knows it's an estimate, not a verified figure from the accounting system. Example: total_stock_qty = 240 units cost_price = Rs 850 per unit capital_locked = Rs 2,04,000
⚠️
The Rs 1,000 fallback is a known gap If the cost price is not available from either the stock view or the master products table, the system defaults to Rs 1,000 per unit as a guess. For products where the actual cost is significantly different (e.g., a specialised industrial adhesive at Rs 8,500/unit), this severely underestimates the capital at risk and could cause management to underweight the urgency of clearing that item. This is flagged for improvement: the master products table should always have a cost_price column, and it should be a mandatory non-null field.
08

Campaign List Export

inventory.py → st.download_button()

The full target buyer list — both past buyers and lookalike partners — can be downloaded as a CSV with one click, named automatically after the selected product. This is the operational handoff from the dashboard to the sales team: the manager exports the list, forwards it to the relevant repswith their targets, and the calls can begin without any separate CRM setup.

Export columns (all available columns that exist): ["potential_buyer", "mobile_no", "buyer_past_purchase_qty", "Audience Type", "last_purchase_date"] Filename pattern: leads_{product_name_with_underscores}.csv e.g.: leads_Epoxy_Primer_1L.csv Audience Type column tells the sales rep: "Past Buyer" → "You've sold this to them before — warm call, easy open" "Lookalike (Cluster: VIP Stable)" → "Similar profile to buyers, first time pitch this product" Mobile number column: directly actionable for WhatsApp or phone outreach. Lookalike rows show "Lookalike" in mobile column — rep must look up number separately.

10

Limitations & Improvements

#LimitationImpactImprovement path
1 Cost price fallback to Rs 1,000 Capital Locked metric is wrong for products not in the master catalogue. Finance decisions based on an incorrect capital figure could under-prioritise urgent clearance. Enforce a non-null cost_price field in master_products. Add a data quality check that flags products in stock_stats with no matching master record.
2 Stock view can be up to 24 hours stale A product sold this morning might still appear as "240 units dead stock" if the view refreshes nightly. A clearance campaign might launch for stock that's already sold. Trigger a view refresh on demand (a "Refresh Stock Data" button in the UI) or switch to a streaming view for warehouse data where real-time accuracy matters.
3 Lookalike mobile numbers not available Lookalike partners show "Lookalike" as their mobile number — the sales rep has to look up the contact number separately, adding friction to the outreach workflow. Join the lookalike results back to the partner master table to include the mobile number. This is a straightforward data join that requires partner contact info to be in the system.
4 No discount recommendation engine The manager must decide what discount to offer. A 10% discount might not move 300-day-old stock, but a 30% discount might clear it in a week. Add a discount calculator: given the product's daily holding cost (interest rate × capital locked ÷ 365), compute the break-even discount at which it's better to clear at a loss than continue holding. Show this as the "minimum viable clearance price."
5 No campaign outcome tracking There's no way to know whether the exported campaign list resulted in sales. The module surfaces leads but doesn't close the loop. After a clearance campaign runs, check if the partner appeared in new transactions for that product within 14 days. Build a "clearance conversion rate" metric per campaign — this teaches the system which audience types (past buyers vs lookalikes) convert better.
