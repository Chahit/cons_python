01

Why Clustering Matters — And What Breaks Without It

Imagine you want to benchmark a small regional distributor in Rajasthan who buys ₹3L/month mainly in feature phones. Without clustering, you'd compare them to every partner in the database — including metro key accounts buying ₹50L/month across 12 product categories. The "gap" numbers would be enormous, irrelevant, and would destroy any trust the rep has in the tool.

Clustering solves this. By grouping partners based on how they buy (which categories, in what proportions, how often, how regularly), we ensure that peer comparisons are always like-for-like. The benchmark for our Rajasthan feature-phone distributor is 12 other distributors who behave exactly like them — not the national key accounts.

Here are all the downstream features that depend on clustering working correctly:

FeatureDepends on ClusteringWhat breaks if clusters are wrong
Peer Gap AnalysisCompares a partner's spend to cluster peersGap numbers are inflated or meaningless
Cross-Sell RecommendationsBuilt from peer gap signals per clusterWrong products recommended — sales rep loses trust

The 7 Feature Groups

clustering_mixin.py → _build_cluster_features()

The engine builds features from 7 distinct behavioural signals. These are grouped by their "namespace" prefix (mix::, rfm::, vel::, etc.) and combined into a single feature matrix before clustering begins.

Each group gets a weight that reflects how important it is for distinguishing partner types. These weights are applied during the PCA compression step — more important groups contribute proportionally more to the final embedding.

Recency-Weighted Category Mix (The Key Formula)

The category mix is not computed from lifetime spend. It uses a recency-weighted combination of three time windows — ensuring that what a partner bought last month matters more than what they bought two years ago:

For each time window, compute category share of spend: mix_90d = category spend in last 90 days ÷ total spend in last 90 days mix_180d = category spend in last 180 days ÷ total spend in last 180 days mix_365d = category spend in last 365 days ÷ total spend in last 365 days Recency-weighted combination: mix::rw::{category} = 0.5 × mix_90d + 0.3 × mix_180d + 0.2 × mix_365d Result: a partner who just started buying smartphones will reflect that in their mix features even if they bought mostly accessories for 2 years before.

This means clusters update naturally when a partner shifts their buying behaviour — no manual re-tagging required.

Scale: Portfolio Concentration Index

Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) — common in economics to measure market concentration portfolio_concentration = Σ(category_share²) Range: 1/N (perfectly diversified) to 1.0 (all spend in one category) A partner spending 100% in mobiles → concentration = 1.0 A partner split equally across 5 categories → concentration = 0.20

Seasonality: Seasonal Amplitude

seasonal_amplitude = (max_quarterly_spend - min_quarterly_spend) ÷ mean_quarterly_spend High amplitude = strong seasonal buying pattern (festival-driven distributor) Low amplitude = steady year-round buyer (everyday products) Also captures which quarter is dominant via one-hot encoding (season_q1 → season_q4)
03

Shannon Entropy — Measuring Category Diversity

clustering_mixin.py → _compute_category_entropy()

Shannon entropy is borrowed from information theory. It tells us how "spread out" a partner's spending is across categories. We use it as a diversity signal inside the clustering features — not just as a label, but as a numeric value that helps the algorithm understand how concentrated or diversified a buyer's portfolio is.

Shannon entropy formula: H = -Σ [ p_i × log₂(p_i) ] where p_i = share of category i in total spend Normalised version (0 to 1 scale): H_norm = H ÷ log₂(N) where N = number of product categories Examples: Partner buys 100% mobiles only → H = 0 (no diversity) Partner splits evenly across 4 categories → H ≈ 2.0 (H_norm = 1.0) Partner: 70% mobiles, 20% accessories, 10% telecom → H ≈ 1.16 (H_norm ≈ 0.73)
🔍
Why not just count the number of categories? A partner who spends ₹9.5L in mobiles and ₹0.5L each in 3 other categories looks very diverse by count (4 categories) but is actually highly concentrated. Entropy captures this nuance — the more unequal the distribution, the lower the entropy score. This is the same technique used by economists to measure market concentration (Herfindahl index). Entropy is the information-theoretic equivalent.
04

Network Co-Purchase Similarity

clustering_mixin.py → _compute_network_features()

This feature group treats partners as nodes in a network, connected by how similar their product portfolios are. It asks: "Does this partner have a unique buying pattern, or do they look like many others?"

Steps: 1. Build a spend vector for each partner (one dimension per product category) 2. Normalise each vector to unit length (convert to direction, ignore scale) 3. Compute cosine similarity between every pair of partners cosine_similarity(A, B) = (A · B) ÷ (|A| × |B|) = 1.0 if identical category mix = 0.0 if completely non-overlapping Derived network features per partner: net_mean_peer_sim = average cosine similarity to top-5 most similar partners net_max_peer_sim = highest cosine similarity to any single partner net_centrality = mean cosine similarity to ALL partners (network "hub" score)
💡
What does a high centrality score mean in business terms? A partner with high network centrality buys roughly what most other partners buy — they're a "typical" distributor. This partner is a safe candidate for standard recommendations (what works for most works for them). A partner with low centrality has a unique buying pattern — standard recommendations may not apply, and they need more customised attention. The clustering algorithm will often assign these low-centrality partners to the "Uncategorised/Outlier" bucket.
05

PCA → UMAP: Dimensionality Reduction Pipeline

clustering_mixin.py → _compress_features_weighted_pca_umap()

Before clustering, we have one problem: too many features. With 7 feature groups and potentially 30–50 columns, most clustering algorithms struggle — this is called the "curse of dimensionality." Distance metrics become meaningless in very high dimensions (every point looks equally far from every other point). The solution is to compress features into a lower-dimensional space that preserves the important structure.

Step 1: Group-Weighted PCA

PCA (Principal Component Analysis) runs separately within each feature group, then the outputs are scaled by the group's business priority weight before being concatenated.

For each feature group (e.g., mix::, rfm::, vel::) 1. Standardise using RobustScaler (resistant to outliers) 2. Run PCA to find principal components 3. n_components = min(n_features, n_partners÷6, 8) 4. Multiply PCA output by group weight Concatenate all group PCA outputs → combined weighted feature matrix Why RobustScaler vs StandardScaler? StandardScaler uses mean ± std → sensitive to outliers RobustScaler uses median ± IQR → a single partner with ₹5Cr spend won't drag all other scale:: features to near-zero
🔍
Why run PCA per group instead of on everything at once? If you run a single PCA on all 50 features, the components end up being arbitrary linear combinations of every feature type. A principal component might capture "high RFM recency + high mobile spend + low gap_cv" all at once — which means the group weights can't be applied afterwards. By running PCA per group and then weighting, we preserve the business priority structure.

Step 2: UMAP — Non-Linear Compression

After group-weighted PCA, we have a reduced but still multi-dimensional matrix. UMAP (Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection) compresses this further into 15 dimensions while preserving the non-linear structure of the data — the "shapes" that PCA flattens out.

Target output: 15 UMAP dimensions (configurable) Key parameters — both adaptive to dataset size: n_neighbors = max(5, min(30, √n_partners)) Controls local vs global structure balance Too low (n=3): noisy, captures only micro-local structure Too high (n=50): blurs boundaries, clusters merge together √n is the theoretical sweet spot from the UMAP paper Examples: 100 partners → n=10, 400 partners → n=20 min_dist: < 50 partners → 0.10 (spread out — avoid over-compression) 50–200 → 0.05 (balanced) 200+ → 0.01 (tighter — needs more separation in dense data)
⚖️
What if UMAP is not installed? The system detects a missing umap-learn package and falls back gracefully to using the weighted PCA output directly (pip install umap-learn to enable). Clustering still works in this fallback mode — it's just less able to capture non-linear cluster shapes. For datasets under 200 partners, the fallback is nearly equivalent.

HDBSCAN vs GMM — Algorithm Competition

clustering_mixin.py → _run_clustering()

Two fundamentally different clustering algorithms run on the same UMAP-compressed features. The one with the better Silhouette Score wins and its cluster assignments are used for all downstream logic.

AlgorithmHow it worksStrengthsWeaknessBest for
HDBSCAN
(Hierarchical Density-Based)		 Finds dense regions of data separated by sparse regions. Partners far from any dense region become "outliers" (cluster = -1) No need to specify cluster count. Finds outliers naturally. Works on non-convex (curved) cluster shapes. Can produce too many small clusters on noisy data. Outlier rate can be high. Large partner bases with natural outliers (unusual buying patterns)
GMM
(Gaussian Mixture Model)		 Assumes data comes from K Gaussian distributions (soft boundaries). Partners can partially belong to multiple clusters. Smooth probabilistic boundaries. Every point gets assigned (no outliers). Works well for overlapping groups. Requires K clusters to be specified upfront. Assumes clusters are roughly elliptical. Partner bases where buying patterns overlap and transition gradually

The Competition

Both algorithms run on the same UMAP output Winner determined by Silhouette Score: Silhouette Score = (b - a) ÷ max(a, b) a = mean intra-cluster distance for this point b = mean distance to nearest other cluster's points Range: -1 (worst) to +1 (best). > 0.4 is considered good. if silhouette(HDBSCAN) > silhouette(GMM): Use HDBSCAN result else: Use GMM result
ℹ️
Why run two algorithms instead of just picking one? No single clustering algorithm is universally best. HDBSCAN excels when clusters are dense and well-separated; GMM excels when clusters overlap and boundaries are fuzzy. B2B partner data can show either pattern depending on how many partners there are and how diverse the product mix is. Running both and picking the winner ensures we always get the best result for this specific dataset on this specific day — not a fixed algorithm choice that may have been right 6 months ago but isn't right today.

Outlier Partner Reassignment

HDBSCAN often marks 10–30% of partners as outliers (cluster = -1). Just displaying "Uncategorised" for 30% of your partner base is not useful. The system applies a smart reassignment logic:

For each outlier partner: 1. Compute Euclidean distance to centroid of every non-outlier cluster 2. Find the nearest cluster centroid Reassign IF distance < 90th percentile of intra-cluster distances × 1.15 (within 15% of the normal reach of the nearest cluster) High-value outliers (revenue > 70th percentile) get a larger radius: distance multiplier = 1.50 instead of 1.15 (we're more aggressive about giving VIP-adjacent partners a cluster) Partners still outside this range → labelled "Uncategorised" (unique behaviour)
07

VIP vs Growth Tier Classification

clustering_mixin.py → _assign_cluster_tiers()

After clusters are formed, every cluster (and every partner within it) is assigned a tier: VIP or Growth. This tier determines which archetype labels are available and how the partner is treated in the recommendation engine.

A cluster qualifies as VIP if ALL three conditions are met: 1. Cluster's average revenue ≥ 80th percentile of ALL cluster average revenues → The cluster must be in the top 20% by spend level 2. Cluster contributes BETWEEN 20% and 45% of total revenue → VIP=20% ensures they're commercially significant → VIP=45% cap prevents the "everyone is VIP" problem 3. No hard overrides from governance rules (configurable per deployment) All other clusters → Growth tier Individual partners within a Growth cluster can still show VIP-level spend → These become "Rising VIPs" in the label matching step
🔍
Why the 20–45% revenue share range? Without a minimum, clusters with 2 large partners might get VIP status even when those 2 partners are outliers. Without a maximum, in a small network a single dominant cluster might consume 70% of revenue — calling everyone there "VIP" makes the label meaningless. The 20–45% window ensures VIP clusters are commercially meaningful but not the whole network.

Business Archetype Labelling

clustering_mixin.py → _generate_cluster_labels_heuristic()

The old system produced labels like "VIP-0" and "Growth-2." Meaningless to a sales manager. The current system maps every cluster to a recognisable business archetype based on signals extracted from the cluster centroid profile.

How Archetype Matching Works

For each cluster, compute a text profile summary from centroid signals: spend level: High-spend | Medium-spend | Low-spend breadth: diversified | moderate | focused concentration: highly concentrated | well-distributed Match profile against archetype priority rules (first match wins): VIP tier: "High-spend + diversified" → Strategic Accounts "High-spend + highly concentrated" → Category Champions "High-spend + moderate" → Core Revenue Drivers "High-spend + focused" → Anchor Specialists "Medium-spend + diversified" → Emerging Power Buyers "Medium-spend" → Steady Contributors "Low-spend" (VIP edge case) → Rising VIPs Growth tier: "High-spend + diversified" → High-Growth Partners "High-spend + highly concentrated" → Niche Power Players "High-spend" → High-Volume Accounts "Medium-spend + diversified" → Balanced Growth Partners "Medium-spend + focused" → Category Growth Specialists "Medium-spend" → Mid-Tier Growing Accounts "Low-spend" → Win-Back Targets

OpenAI-Enhanced Labels (Optional)

If an OpenAI API key is present, the system sends each cluster's centroid profile to GPT and requests richer, context-aware label suggestions. These replace the heuristic labels when available. Validation rules ensure GPT-generated labels are between 3 and 80 characters and contain no JSON formatting artifacts.

Cluster Quality Validation

clustering_mixin.py → _compute_quality_scores() + _estimate_stability()

Every time clustering runs, the system automatically evaluates how good the result is. If quality drops below threshold, it logs a warning. Managers can see these scores in the Monitoring tab.

Silhouette Score
> 0.4
Measures how well-separated clusters are from each other. Range: -1 to +1. Above 0.4 is considered good for real-world B2B data.
Calinski-Harabasz
Higher = Better
Ratio of between-cluster variance to within-cluster variance. Tells you if clusters are compact and well-separated simultaneously.
ARI Stability
≈ 1.0
Runs clustering on 5 random 80% subsets of partners. ARI = 1 means results are identical every time. Unstable clusters mean something is wrong with the data or parameters.
Stability test (Bootstrap ARI): for each of 5 bootstrap runs: sample = 80% of partners (random, no replacement) cluster the sample using the same algorithm compare_labels = align to base clustering using Hungarian algorithm compute ARI(base_labels[sample], compare_labels) stability_score = mean(ARI across 5 runs) ARI (Adjusted Rand Index): corrects for chance agreement ARI = 1.0 → perfect match every time ARI = 0.0 → no better than random ARI < 0.3 → unstable clusters, flag for review
🔒
Why is stability important for a business? If cluster assignments change significantly every week, sales reps get confused — a partner who was "Strategic Account" last Monday might be "Win-Back Target" this Monday for no apparent reason. Stable clusters build trust. The stability ARI score lets the engineering team detect when data quality or business changes are causing the model to become inconsistent before it causes a field problem.

Feature Drift Detection

clustering_mixin.py → _compute_feature_drift()

The system keeps a lightweight baseline of the mean and standard deviation of every feature from the previous clustering run. On each new run, it computes a Z-score shift for every feature to detect if the data has changed significantly.

mean_abs_z_shift = mean of |( current_feature_mean - baseline_feature_mean ) ÷ baseline_feature_std| Interpretation: z_shift < 0.5 → normal variation, no concern z_shift 0.5–1.0 → moderate shift, investigate data pipeline z_shift > 1.0 → significant data change — may indicate: - New batch of partners added - Seasonal shift in buying patterns - Data pipeline issue (missing transactions)

The baseline updates on every run, so the system adapts gradually to real business changes rather than continuously alerting on slow drift. A sudden spike in z_shift (e.g., from 0.3 to 2.1 in one run) is the signal worth investigating — that's what indicates a data problem vs a business trend.
