Market Basket Analysis is the science of finding patterns in purchasing behaviour. Its core question is: "If a partner buys Product A, what else are they likely to buy?" The system mines these patterns from transaction history, ranks their reliability, estimates their revenue impact, and hands the rep a ready-to-deliver pitch script — all in under 2 seconds. This module also includes two advanced techniques: sequential pattern mining (what they buy next) and cross-category upgrade detection (when partners expand from one category to another).

01

Why Market Basket Analysis?

In physical retail, this technique is famous for discovering things like "people who buy nappies on Friday evenings also buy beer" — which led to stores placing those items near each other. In our B2B context, the insight is similar but commercially more valuable: "Partners who buy smartphones almost always also buy smartphone accessories in the same month."

There are three ways this insight is used in the dashboard:

Use CaseWhere it showsBusiness value
Global rules tableMarket Basket tab — all partner viewGives the sales team a product catalogue with built-in cross-sell intelligence
Partner-specific recommendationsMarket Basket + Partner 360"This specific partner buys X but not Y — pitch Y" based on what their cluster peers do
Affinity Bundle recommendationsRecommendation HubHigh-confidence rules feed directly into the recommendation engine as primary actions
🔍
Why use mining rather than just asking the sales team what goes together? Sales teams have strong intuition for their top 5–10 products. But a typical distribution catalogue has 50–200 products across 8–15 categories. No human can reliably track which of the 4,950+ possible product pairs actually co-occur frequently enough to be reliable cross-sell signals. Mining discovers patterns from data that human intuition would miss — especially in long-tail categories.
02

The Three Core Metrics — Support, Confidence, Lift

Every association rule in the system is described by three numbers. Understanding these three is the key to reading the Market Basket table correctly.

Support
N
How many baskets (partner-month combinations) contained this product pair. A raw count, not a percentage. Default minimum: 5 baskets.
Confidence
%
P(Buy B | Buy A). "Of all the times partners bought A, what % also bought B?" Default minimum: 15%. The primary reliability signal.
Lift
×
How much better than random is this pairing? Lift = 1.0 means no affinity. Lift > 1.0 means genuinely correlated. Default minimum: 1.0×.
The exact formulas computed in PostgreSQL: confidence_a_to_b = times_bought_together ÷ support_a support_a = number of baskets containing Product A confidence_b_to_a = times_bought_together ÷ support_b (stored for bidirectional analysis but not yet shown in UI) lift_a_to_b = confidence_a_to_b ÷ (support_b ÷ total_baskets) = P(A∩B) ÷ (P(A) × P(B)) if baskets are the unit Where total_baskets = count(distinct partner_id + sale_month combinations)

Reading this: Of all the months when a partner bought Smartphones, 73% of those months they also bought Accessories. ⚖️
Why does Lift matter if Confidence is already high? A product bought by 95% of all partners (e.g., SIM cards) will show high confidence in almost every rule — not because of affinity, but because it's universally purchased. Lift corrects for this by dividing by P(B) — if B is bought 80% of the time anyway, a rule with 85% confidence is not impressive. But if B is bought only 20% of the time and your rule shows 60% confidence, Lift = 3.0 — that's a real signal.
03

How Association Rules Are Mined — The SQL Pipeline

associations_mixin.py → _load_associations_with_metrics()

The primary mining method uses a 5-step SQL query rather than a Python library. This is a deliberate design choice — it keeps the computation inside the database engine (which is already optimised for set operations) rather than pulling millions of rows into memory.

SQL CTE StepWhat it computesWhy this step?
max_date_cte Maximum approved transaction date Ensures lookback window anchors on actual data, not today's date (handles data gaps gracefully)
baskets Distinct (partner, month, product) triples within the lookback window Defines what a "basket" is: one partner's purchases in one calendar month. Monthly granularity prevents count inflation from a partner buying the same product daily.
basket_total / product_support Total basket count; per-product basket count Denominators needed to compute confidence and lift accurately
pair_support Self-join of baskets on same partner + same month, product A < product B Finds co-occurring pairs. The A < B condition prevents duplicate counting (A→B and B→A counted once)
avg_product_value / avg_product_margin Average revenue and margin per line item for each product Enables financial estimates: how much money does this rule actually generate?
💡
Why SQL instead of the mlxtend FP-Growth library? The mlxtend library loads the full transaction matrix into Python memory as a one-hot encoded DataFrame. For 500 partners × 200 products × 12 months = 1.2M cells, this can use 500MB+ of RAM before any computation starts. The SQL approach computes pair counts directly in the database — only the result set (typically 200–2,000 rows) is transferred to Python. This trades Python flexibility for a 10–50× memory reduction, which matters on shared deployment environments.

Basket Definition — Why Monthly?

basket = DISTINCT (party_id, TO_CHAR(date, 'YYYY-MM'), product_name) A "basket" = one partner's purchase activity for one calendar month. Alternative considered: daily baskets (each invoice = one basket) Problem: a partner placing 15 small daily orders in January would count as 15 separate baskets. If they buy Product A every day and Product B once, the confidence is artificially diluted. Monthly granularity treats the full month's activity as a single purchasing decision — which better reflects how B2B distributors actually replenish their stock. Lookback window: configurable via MBA_LOOKBACK_MONTHS (.env), default = 12 months
04

Adaptive Min-Support Formula

associations_mixin.py → _compute_adaptive_min_support()

Every association mining algorithm needs a minimum support threshold — a cut-off below which a product pair is considered too rare to be reliable. Set it too high, and you miss valid patterns. Set it too low, and spurious one-off coincidences dominate the results.

The old system used a fixed min_support = 2%. This is fine for a grocery retailer with 10,000 transactions. In a B2B distribution network with 200 partners and 50 products buying once a month, the average pair occurs in only 4–8 baskets — meaning 2% would require at least 2–4 occurrences per pair, which might eliminate many real patterns.

Step 1: Measure the actual sparsity of the data sparsity = actual_co-occurrences ÷ max_possible_co-occurrences = Σ(pairs seen > 0) ÷ (n_products × (n_products-1) ÷ 2) Step 2: Choose min_support based on sparsity band sparsity < 0.10 → min_support = max(0.005, 2 ÷ n_baskets) (Very sparse: require at least 2 real co-occurrences) sparsity 0.10–0.30 → min_support = 0.01 (Moderate density: standard B2B range) sparsity > 0.30 → min_support = 0.02 (Dense data: safe to use retail-style default) Floor guarantee: min_support ≥ 2/n_baskets = "at least 2 real occurrences" This prevents rules like "Product A and B were ordered together exactly once" from appearing as reliable cross-sell recommendations.
ℹ️
What does sparsity mean in practice? If you have 50 products, the maximum possible distinct pairs is 50×49÷2 = 1,225. If only 120 of these pairs were ever bought together, sparsity = 120/1,225 = 9.8%. That's under 10% — so the system will use the ultra-low support threshold (2/n_baskets) to avoid discarding the patterns that actually exist. A grocery retailer with 1,000 products and 100,000 daily transactions might have 60% pair sparsity — there the standard 2% threshold is appropriate.
05

Rule Strength Classification

associations_mixin.py → _decorate_rule_quality()

Every rule is tagged as High, Medium, or Low strength based on both confidence and lift together — not just one of them. A rule high in one but low in the other may not be as reliable as it appears.

rule_strength: "High" = confidence ≥ 0.40 AND lift ≥ 1.5 Reliable and meaningfully above random. Pitch with confidence. "Medium" = confidence ≥ 0.20 AND lift ≥ 1.1 Decent signal. Useful cross-sell but validate with the partner. "Low" = everything else Weak signal. Might be coincidental. Use only if no better options exist.

Low Support Flag

low_support_flag = True if support_a < min_support OR support_b < min_support = False otherwise By default: low-support rules are hidden (include_low_support = False) Checkbox in the UI allows showing them for research/exploration purposes Low-support rules are tagged but not removed when the checkbox is on — so the rep can see them but knows to treat them as tentative signals.
06

Revenue & Margin Gain Estimates

associations_mixin.py SQL → avg_product_value + avg_product_margin CTEs

The engine doesn't just tell you that Product B is frequently bought with Product A — it tells you how much extra revenue and margin that cross-sell generates per occurrence, per week, per month, and per year.

Base gain estimations (computed in SQL): expected_revenue_gain = times_bought_together × avg_line_revenue(Product B) expected_margin_gain = times_bought_together × avg_line_margin(Product B) margin_rate = avg_line_margin(B) ÷ avg_line_revenue(B) (clipped 2%–60%) Where avg_line_margin = avg(net_amt - transfer_price × qty) over lookback period Weekly / Monthly / Yearly projections (Python-side post-processing): expected_gain_weekly = expected_revenue_gain × (7 ÷ lookback_days) expected_gain_monthly = expected_revenue_gain × (30 ÷ lookback_days) expected_gain_yearly = expected_revenue_gain × (365 ÷ lookback_days) (Same formula applied to margin columns)
⚠️
Important limitation on gain estimates The gain estimate is based on how often the pair was bought together historically — not on how many new sales could be made. If Product A and B were bought together 87 times in 12 months, the gain is estimated as if those 87 co-purchases all had an incremental B sale. In reality, some partners would have bought B anyway (no rep influence needed). The actual incremental gain is some fraction of this number — likely 30–60% if the cross-sell is well-targeted. The estimates are a ceiling, useful for prioritisation but not for P&L forecasting.

The Sales Script Generator

The best association rule is automatically converted into a three-part sales conversation script. No AI API, no manual writing — it's a template with the real product names, confidence numbers, and gain estimates filled in.

Opening Line
"We noticed you regularly order [Product A]. Most of our partners who buy this also pick up [Product B] at the same time — shall I add it to today's order?"

Sequential Pattern Mining

associations_mixin.py → mine_sequential_patterns()

Standard association rules are "simultaneous" — they find what's bought together in the same month. Sequential patterns find what partners tend to buy afterwards, typically 7–90 days later. This is a fundamentally different and valuable signal.

Example: A partner who just ordered a new product category for the first time tends to follow up with accessories from that category in the next 30 days. Standard MBA wouldn't catch this because the purchases are in different months.

Sequential pattern definition: Partner buys Product A in month M Partner buys Product B in month M+1 (within max_gap_days) → This is an "A then B" pattern confidence_a_then_b = count(partners with "A then B") ÷ count(partners who bought A) lift = same formula as standard — corrects for random chance Key difference from standard MBA: Standard: A and B in the SAME basket (same month) Sequential: A first, then B in a LATER basket (next N days)
💡
Business use case for sequential patterns When a distributor is onboarded to a new product category, they typically place a trial order first. Sequential mining tells you: "Of every partner who placed a trial Smartphones order, 68% placed an Accessories order within 30 days." Armed with this, the rep can proactively call with an Accessories offer 15 days after the Smartphones trial — before the partner even knows they need it. This turns reactive order-taking into proactive demand generation.

Parameters

Cross-Category Upgrade Detection

associations_mixin.py → mine_cross_category_upgrades()

This is the most strategically valuable of the three mining methods. It answers the question: "Which partners in Category X tend to eventually expand into Category Y?" — and critically, which ones haven't yet.

Example: Partners who buy Telecom equipment tend to start buying Battery & Power products within 60 days. If you have 30 Telecom partners who haven't yet bought Power products — you have 30 warm leads already identified.

Upgrade pattern definition: Partner is active in Category X (at least 1 purchase in last gap_days) Partner buys from Category Y for the FIRST TIME within gap_days confidence = count(X→Y upgrades) ÷ count(partners active in X) lift = confidence ÷ (count(Y buyers) ÷ total_active_partners) Output: for each upgrade pattern (category X → category Y): - upgrade_count: how many times this upgrade happened - partners_in_x: how many partners are currently active in X (potential converts) - partner_names: the specific partners who showed this pattern (for targeting)

Temporal Decay in Enhanced Mining

The enhanced FP-Growth tab applies a recency weight to transaction data before running the algorithm. More recent purchases count more than older ones — reflecting that a pattern from 18 months ago may not reflect current buying behaviour.

decay_weight = e^(-λ × days_since_purchase) λ (lambda) controls how fast older purchases lose weight Higher λ = stronger recency bias Lower λ = more weight on historical patterns Applied to basket frequency before FP-Growth itemset counting, so recent baskets contribute more to support/confidence calculations.
