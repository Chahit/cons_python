Most companies evaluate their sales reps by revenue alone. That's one-dimensional — a rep who generates ₹2Cr by visiting 5 major accounts while spending ₹80,000 in field expenses is very different from a rep who generates ₹80L by visiting 40 small partners while spending ₹8,000. This module measures six dimensions simultaneously: revenue generation, order volume, partner coverage breadth, field tour activity, expense efficiency, and after-sales issue management. Together, they give management a complete picture — not just who's selling the most, but who's doing it most efficiently.

Data Architecture — Six Source Tables

sales_rep_mixin.py → ensure_sales_rep_data()

Unlike every other module which queries a single primary table, the Sales Rep engine joins data from six separate operational tables — each tracking a different aspect of a rep's field activity. Each table is fetched separately, aggregated per user, and then merged into a single leaderboard row per rep.

🔗
Why join transactions_dsr AND transactions_dsr_products separately? The transactions_dsr table contains order headers (one row per visit/invoice). The revenue isn't stored at the header level — it's in the product line items (transactions_dsr_products). If we just aggregated on the header, we'd only get order counts. By joining on id ↔ dsr_id, we get the actual revenue per product line, then sum it. This is the correct way to compute revenue — it handles multi-product orders properly.
02

Active Employee Filter

sales_rep_mixin.py → lines 36–38

The leaderboard shows only currently active employees. This is enforced by filtering the auth_user table at the very start of the pipeline, before any joins. This matters because former employees who closed large deals before leaving would otherwise appear at the top of a naive revenue sort, giving a misleading picture of who to actually invest coaching resources into.

Active status check (normalised to handle booleans stored as strings): users["is_active"] = users["is_active"].astype(str).str.lower() users = users[users["is_active"].isin(["true", "1", "yes"])] Why normalise? PostgreSQL boolean columns often serialise to Python as "True" (capital T), "1", or "true" depending on the DB driver version. Straightforward bool comparison would miss the string variants and accidentally include all users. Additional filter: only reps who have DONE something are shown: df = df[ (total_orders > 0) | (total_tours > 0) | (total_expenses > 0) ] Excludes admin/manager accounts in auth_user who have user IDs but never appear in sales data — they'd show as Rs 0 / 0 orders leaderboard entries.
03

Revenue Attribution — Who Gets Credit?

Revenue is attributed to the user_id field on each transaction in transactions_dsr. This is the rep who entered or is assigned to the order — typically the field rep who visited the partner that day.

Revenue aggregation (Python-side, after join): tx_merged = transactions_dsr.merge( transactions_dsr_products, left_on="id", right_on="dsr_id", how="left" ) "left join" ensures we don't drop orders with no product lines (returns, cancellations) rep_sales = tx_merged.groupby("user_id").agg( total_orders = ("id_x", "nunique"), ← unique order IDs per rep total_revenue = ("net_amt", "sum"), ← total of all line-item net amounts unique_customers = ("party_id", "nunique") ← distinct partner companies served ) Why nunique(id_x) and not count()? After the join, each header row is duplicated once per product line. An order with 10 products would count as 10 if we used count(). nunique() of the header ID gives the correct "1 visit = 1 order" count.
04

True ROI — Revenue Per Rupee Spent

sales_rep_mixin.py → lines 117–121

The most distinctive metric in this module is the True ROI — the ratio of total revenue generated to total expenses claimed. It answers: "For every rupee this rep spends on field activity, how many rupees in revenue do they bring back?"

revenue_roi = total_revenue ÷ total_expenses ← if expenses > 0 = 9999 (sentinel) ← if revenue > 0 but no expenses (pure efficiency) = 0.0 ← if no revenue AND no expenses Displayed in UI as: min(int(revenue_roi), 9999) + "x" Cap at 9999 prevents a rep who never filed expenses from showing "∞x" which would obscure meaningful comparisons. 9999x is the sentinel for "no expense data". Examples: Rep A: Revenue ₹50L, Expenses ₹25,000 → ROI = 200x Rep B: Revenue ₹2Cr, Expenses ₹5,00,000 → ROI = 40x Rep B earns more total revenue, but Rep A is 5× more efficient per rupee spent.
⚠️
ROI alone is not a complete performance signal A rep who works only in their home city (no travel expenses) and exclusively visits nearby VIP partners will always show a very high ROI. A rep who covers remote rural territories with high travel costs may show a low ROI even though they're doing more difficult, high-impact work. ROI should always be read alongside geographic territory context and unique_customers served. This is a known limitation not yet present in the UI.

Expense vs Revenue Scatter — The ROI Chart

The Expense vs Revenue Yield scatter plot maps every rep on two axes, with bubble size proportional to unique_customers served. This creates four natural quadrants:

🟡 High Expense, High Revenue
Good performers with above-average spend. Investigate whether expenses can be reduced without impacting revenue — territory clustering or smarter route planning.
🟢 Low Expense, High Revenue
Top performers. Study their methods. These reps have found efficient approaches worth replicating across the team.
🟣 High Expense, Low Revenue
Needs investigation. Possibly covering difficult territories, or activity not converting to orders. Requires coaching or territory restructuring.
🔵 Low Expense, Low Revenue
Low activity overall. Either new joiner still ramping, or underperforming. Needs a performance plan or tighter manager oversight.

Revenue Efficiency Ratios

sales_rep_performance.py → Revenue Efficiency Analysis section

The efficiency table breaks down revenue into three per-unit ratios, helping managers understand quality of activity rather than just volume.

avg_revenue_per_order = total_revenue ÷ total_orders How much revenue does one visit/invoice typically bring? High value = rep is effectively pitching premium products or bundles Low value = lots of small, low-margin orders avg_revenue_per_partner = total_revenue ÷ unique_customers Average revenue per partner relationship this rep holds Reps with high rev/partner are extracting full wallet share from their accounts Reps with low rev/partner may be under-selling partners who could buy more avg_cost_per_order = total_expenses ÷ total_orders Expense burden per selling event High cost/order = either few orders per trip, or expensive territory Ideal: low cost/order AND high revenue/order = high-quality sales efficiency All three are NaN-safe: divide-by-zero → 0 (fillna after division)
💡
How to use the efficiency table in a coaching session Compare avg_revenue_per_partner across reps. If Rep A earns ₹1.8L per partner and Rep B earns ₹40K per partner but they have similar partner counts, Rep B is systematically under-selling. Check: is Rep B selling fewer SKUs? Missing cross-sell opportunities? The Market Basket module's partner-specific recommendations would be the first tool to give Rep B during a coaching session.

Per-Rep Revenue Forecast — Prophet + Linear Fallback

sales_rep_mixin.py → get_sales_rep_monthly_revenue()

When drilling into an individual rep, the system generates a forward revenue forecast using the same Prophet → linear regression fallback strategy used for Product Lifecycle forecasting.

Data preparation: rep_tx = all transactions attributed to rep (user_id match) monthly = rep_tx.groupby(YYYY-MM period).sum(net_amt) Forecast method selection: IF len(monthly) ≥ 4 months of history: → Attempt Prophet forecast (min threshold lowered from 6 to 4 for reps) → yearly_seasonality = True ONLY if ≥ 12 months of history (prevents overfitting seasonality on a short window) → changepoint_prior_scale = 0.15 (slightly higher than 0.10 for products, because rep performance can change more abruptly — promotions, personal events) → interval_width = 0.80 (80% confidence interval) IF Prophet fails OR len < 4 months: → Linear regression fallback (np.polyfit deg=1) → Uncertainty band: ±15% of predicted value (fixed) Output: DataFrame with columns [month, revenue, yhat_lower, yhat_upper, type] type = "Actual" (history) or "Forecast" (future periods) Default forecast_months = 3

Issue Management Analysis — The Underrated Metric

The issues-logged chart compares issues raised against total orders for the top 10 reps by issue count. This highlights an important nuance that pure revenue metrics hide.

ℹ️
Why zero issues can be a red flag, not a good sign In customer-facing operations, some friction is normal. Delivery delays, product quality complaints, billing discrepancies — these happen. A rep with 500 orders and zero issues logged either has a perfectly smooth territory (unlikely), or simply isn't following up with partners post-sale to surface problems early. Partners who feel their complaints aren't registered eventually stop engaging — and the first signal is churn. An issues-to-orders ratio of 2–5% is actually a sign of active, engaged account management.

1–5%Active issue logging. Normal range for a well-managed territory.Monitor trends. Ensure resolution times are tracked.
5–15%High-friction territory OR rep is surfacing systemic problems.Investigate root causes — is this a product quality / logistics issue cluster?
> 15%Unusual. May indicate a damaged partner relationship or systemic operational failure.Immediate management review. Check if issue types are concentrated in one or two partners.

Limitations & Improvements

The current Sales Rep module covers revenue attribution fundamentals well but has several known gaps that a mature sales analytics system would address:

#LimitationImpactImprovement Path
1 No target/quota tracking Leaderboard shows absolute performance only. A rep who hit 110% of a tough quota is comparable to one who hit 90% of an easy quota — both shown by raw revenue. Add a rep_targets DB table. Show attainment % = actual / target. Sort leaderboard by attainment rather than raw revenue.
2 No territory context for ROI A rep covering a remote rural district will always show higher expenses and lower ROI than a city-based rep — structurally, not because of performance. Segment reps by territory tier (Metro / Urban / Rural). Compare ROI within tier, not across all reps globally.
3 Tours not linked to revenue outcomes Tour count is tracked but we can't see: "Did the tour on March 15 result in an order from that partner within 7 days?" Match tourplan.party_id to next 7-day transaction. Build a tour-to-conversion rate per rep as an additional KPI.
4 No churn risk overlay on partner coverage The partners a rep serves may include several at high churn risk — but the rep has no visibility of which of their accounts urgently needs a call. Join df_sales_rep's party_id coverage to the churn scores from the main engine. Show each rep: "You have X partners at >60% churn risk — prioritise these."
5 All-time aggregation — no rolling window A rep who was top performer 2 years ago but has been underperforming for 6 months still looks good on the leaderboard. The 3M revenue forecast partially compensates, but the leaderboard itself needs a date range filter. Add a "Date Range" slider (Last 90 days / Last 6M / Last 12M / All Time). Default to 90 days to reflect current performance, not historical tenure.
⚠️
Sensitivity reminder This module contains individual-level performance data. It should only be accessible to users with appropriate HR/management permissions. Consider adding Streamlit role-based access control or a PIN-gate before making this tab visible to all users of the dashboard.
